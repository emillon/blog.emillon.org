What is your company going to make?

Cloverfeed is a website and a mobile app. A user gets a list of items corresponding to what he is most likely to read right now. They are coming from their RSS, Facebook, Twitter, and social news (Reddit, HN). It is spam filtering and priority inbox for all kinds of feeds.

The numbers of items that it displays is proportional to how big a reader you are. From the user’s behavior, we rate how much they liked each piece they read. We then rank and filter future readings. Additionally, Cloverfeed will present you really interesting pieces of news you could have missed with your personal sources. For that, we exploit links between topics and links between people.

What’s new about what you’re making? What substitutes do people resort to because it doesn’t exist yet (or they don’t know about it)?

People spend more time looking for the information they care about (news, being it social or not). They check their feeds (Twitter and Facebook included) or social news sites (HN, Reddit) more often because they fear missing new things (information overload) if they check later. For some part this is because these websites make their revenue through ads: they want to be addictive in order to maximize page views. We want offer them the possibility to pay to be ad-free, and in case of ads: we believe strongly targeted ads are better than more page views.

How do or will you make money? How much could you make? (We realize you can’t know precisely, but give your best estimate.)

We want to have a freemium model. For paying customers, we can provide more import formats (open formats will be free for everybody) and an export format, more information sources… Also, we will probably charge the heavy users of our API.

We think we can get a significant fraction of Twitter users and RSS users. The blooming success of “Google Reader clones” suggests users are willing to switch. The success of Instapaper (1 million users on iOS alone) and of “digests” services suggest there is a market of power-users. A safe guesstimate would be to have 1 million users in one year (from now) and 20 millions soon after. This is not unrealistic as Flipboard, which exists only on mobile devices, has reached 20 million users and 3 billion pageviews in August. A classic freemium model yields 1% of paying customers, so 10,000 users at $15 for the first year and 200,000 users afterwards.

How will you get users? If your idea is the type that faces a chicken-and-egg problem in the sense that it won’t be attractive to users till it has a lot of users (e.g. a marketplace, a dating site, an ad network), how will you overcome that?

We will get users first through word of mouth (HN, Twitter, friends): this way we can iterate on the product while developing a core fanbase.

We don’t face a chicken-and-egg problem in terms of users, but for the added value to kick in, a user needs to spend some time on the site after signing up. For that, we think about doing “users categories”: roughly classifying all of one user’s tastes is quicker than getting their user-level personalization right. We can then leverage (weighted sum parameters of) existing well-defined user profiles.

We will lower the barrier to entry: have RSS import formats, integration of Twitter and Facebook. We would like to integrate (upvotes!) social news sites too (with posts/comments), for Reddit there is an API, the HN API is read-only.
